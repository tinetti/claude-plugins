# Compare & Contrast Framework

Structured evaluation of two or more options. Helps readers make informed decisions by presenting trade-offs honestly rather than picking a winner.

## Best For

- Tool or framework comparisons (Cursor vs Claude Code, Bun vs Node)
- Approach trade-offs (SSR vs CSR, monorepo vs polyrepo)
- Migration decision posts ("Should you switch to X?")
- "I tried both, here's what I found" narratives

## The Steps

### 1. Set the Context

Why is this comparison relevant now? What prompted it?

- A decision you faced or a question you keep hearing
- Establish that both options have merit — avoid strawmanning
- State your perspective and biases upfront

**Example:** "I've been a terminal-first developer for 15 years. That bias is real — keep it in mind as you read."

### 2. Establish Criteria

Define what you're comparing on. This prevents the post from being a feature list.

- Pick 3-5 criteria that actually matter for the decision
- Explain why these criteria matter
- Acknowledge what you're NOT comparing

**Example:** For editor comparison: workflow integration, speed of iteration, learning curve, extensibility.

### 3. Compare on Each Criterion

Walk through each criterion with both options side by side.

- Be specific — show commands, screenshots, or code
- Give each option a fair shake on each point
- Use tables for quick-reference summaries, prose for nuance
- Note where one clearly wins and where it's subjective

### 4. The Nuance Section

This is where the post earns trust. Address the "it depends" honestly.

- Who should choose option A vs B?
- What workflows, team sizes, or constraints tip the balance?
- Are there hybrid approaches?
- What might change this calculus in 6 months?

### 5. Your Take

Share what you actually chose and why — but frame it as personal, not prescriptive.

- Tie back to your stated biases from the opening
- Be specific about your context
- Acknowledge the reader's context may differ

**Example:** "For me, Claude Code wins because I never have to leave my terminal. But if VS Code is home for you, Cursor's inline experience is genuinely great."

## Structure Notes

The strength of this framework is fairness. If the reader can tell which option you prefer by paragraph two, the post loses credibility. Build to your take; don't lead with it.

Tables work well for criterion-by-criterion summaries but should supplement prose, not replace it. The nuance lives in the paragraphs.

## Combination Notes

- **+ The Rashomon:** Instead of comparing options abstractly, present each option as a perspective from a different stakeholder (developer, ops, PM). The comparison becomes multi-dimensional.
- **+ The Sparkline:** Alternate between "what is" (current option's reality) and "what could be" (alternative's promise) for emotional contrast within each criterion.
- **+ Converging Ideas:** Start with seemingly unrelated threads (different projects, different problems) that converge on the same comparison — the reader discovers the trade-off rather than being told it.
- **+ Story Circle:** When the comparison is really about your journey of choosing, use Story Circle as the wrapper with Compare & Contrast for the structured evaluation in the middle.

## When NOT to Use This

If there aren't genuinely two or more valid options, this framework forces a false balance. Also a poor fit when the post is really about your personal journey of choosing — that's a Story Circle post that happens to involve a comparison, not a Compare & Contrast post.
