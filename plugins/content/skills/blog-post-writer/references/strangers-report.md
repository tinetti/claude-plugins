# Stranger's Report Framework

Write as if you're an outsider encountering a system, codebase, or process for the first time. The "fresh eyes" perspective reveals what insiders have normalized. Influenced by anthropological field reports and travel writing — the tradition of making the familiar strange by describing it through someone who has no context for why things are the way they are.

## Best For

- Onboarding narratives ("my first week on the codebase")
- Technology adoption from a newcomer's perspective
- "I joined a new team" stories
- Framework or language exploration posts
- Codebase archaeology — discovering why things are the way they are
- Posts that challenge "we've always done it this way"

## The Steps

### 1. Arrival (Establish Outsider Status)

Declare your newcomer status openly. This isn't weakness — it's your superpower for this post. Ground the reader in the specific moment you arrived: the team, the project, the technology. Show what you expected to find.

**Example:** "I joined the team three weeks ago. My onboarding doc said to clone the repo, run `pnpm install`, and start the dev server. Thirty minutes, it estimated. I'm a senior developer. I've onboarded onto dozens of projects. I expected this to be routine. I want to tell you about what I found instead."

### 2. First Observations (What Stands Out to Fresh Eyes)

Document what surprises you — the weird, the unexpected, the things that clearly made sense to someone but not to you. Be specific. An insider wouldn't notice these things because they've been normalized. That's exactly why you're writing this.

**Example:** "The repo has seven `tsconfig` files. Seven. Each one extends a different base, and two of them extend each other. The `README` mentions a build step but doesn't say what it builds. There's a directory called `legacy` that contains what appears to be the entire original application, still imported by the main app in eleven places. And every component file starts with a comment block that says `// DO NOT EDIT — generated by codegen` but the last codegen run, according to git blame, was 2021."

### 3. Deeper Investigation (Ask Why Things Are the Way They Are)

Move from observation to inquiry. Start pulling threads. Ask the questions that insiders stopped asking years ago. Talk to people. Read old PRs and Slack threads. The investigation should feel genuine — you're not judging, you're trying to understand.

**Example:** "I asked about the seven `tsconfig` files. The answer was illuminating: three different teams had owned this codebase at different times, and each had their own TypeScript conventions. Nobody ever consolidated them because the builds worked. The `legacy` directory? It was supposed to be migrated 'next quarter' — for the last six quarters. Everyone knew. Nobody said it out loud. It was the elephant in the `src` directory."

### 4. The Insider Logic (Discover the Reasons Behind the Strangeness)

This is the empathy step. Show that the weird things aren't stupid — they're artifacts of real decisions made under real constraints. The seven tsconfig files aren't laziness; they're the sedimentary record of three teams' priorities. Understanding the insider logic is what separates a useful report from a complaint.

**Example:** "Once I understood the history, the codebase stopped looking chaotic and started looking like a geological record. Each layer made sense in its era. The `legacy` directory wasn't technical debt from negligence — it was a deliberate choice to keep shipping features instead of pausing for a migration that would've taken months with no visible user impact. The team that made that call was right. The seven teams that inherited the decision were also right not to fix it. Everyone was locally rational. The system was globally strange."

### 5. The Report (Synthesize What Newcomers See That Insiders Miss)

Pull it together. What does the outsider perspective reveal that the insider perspective has lost? This isn't about solutions (though you can offer some). It's about *seeing clearly* — naming the things that have become invisible to the people who work here every day.

**Example:** "Here's what I can see from the outside that's hard to see from the inside: this codebase isn't broken. It's haunted. Every directory is a ghost of a team that used to own it. And the current team navigates around those ghosts so fluently that they've forgotten they're doing it. The biggest risk isn't the technical debt — it's that the workarounds have become invisible. New developers will learn to step around the ghosts without knowing they're there, and the haunting will get quieter without getting better."

## Structure Notes

**Pacing:** Arrival is brief, ~10%. First Observations should be rich and specific, ~25%. Deeper Investigation is ~25%. Insider Logic is the empathy turn, ~20%. The Report is the synthesis, ~20%.

**The outsider voice is the engine.** Every observation should feel like it's being seen for the first time. Avoid phrases like "as we all know" or "obviously." Nothing is obvious to the stranger.

**Empathy is mandatory.** If step 4 doesn't exist — if you skip straight from "this is weird" to "here's what's wrong" — the post becomes arrogant. The insider logic step is what makes this a report instead of a roast.

**Use concrete artifacts.** File names, directory structures, git blame dates, Slack messages, README fragments. The stranger's report gets its power from specific evidence, not general impressions.

## Combination Notes

- **The Rashomon:** The outsider perspective is one view. Combine with insider perspectives for a multi-angle post about the same codebase.
- **Post-Mortem:** The newcomer investigating an incident they weren't present for — reading logs and Slack history to reconstruct what happened.
- **Reverse Chronology:** Discover the history backward through artifacts. Start with what you see today, then peel back layers to find origins.
- **Socratic Path:** Each observation leads to a question, each question leads to a discovery, each discovery leads to another question.

## When NOT to Use This

If you're actually an expert on the system, the outsider framing will feel disingenuous. You can't write a stranger's report about your own codebase — you're not a stranger. Use SCQA or Post-Mortem instead.

If the "weird things" are genuinely bad practices with no redeeming insider logic, this framework's empathy step will feel like excuse-making. Sometimes code is just bad. Use PAS to name the problem and fix it.

Avoid if you joined less than a week ago and haven't done the investigation step. Raw first impressions without understanding are just complaints. The framework needs both the surprise AND the understanding to work.
