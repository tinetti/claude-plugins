# Kishotenketsu Framework

Four-act structure from classical Chinese poetry (qi-cheng-zhuan-he), widely used in Japanese and Korean storytelling. What makes it unique: no conflict required. The twist (Ten) recontextualizes rather than confronts. Where Western frameworks create tension through problems, Kishotenketsu creates it through perspective shifts.

## Best For

- Perspective-shift posts ("I thought X but actually Y")
- Educational posts where insight comes from reframing, not from solving a problem
- "I was wrong but nobody was the villain" posts
- Gentle persuasion — changing the reader's mind without arguing
- Posts about appreciation or understanding something you previously dismissed

## The Steps

### 1. Ki - Introduction (Establish the Subject Plainly)

Introduce the topic simply and directly. No tension, no problem, no hook promising disruption. Just establish what you're talking about and why you're thinking about it.

- Be matter-of-fact — this is observation, not argument
- Set up the reader's initial frame for understanding the topic
- The plainness is strategic: it makes the twist land harder

**Example:** "I've used Vim for about fifteen years. In that time, the question I've been asked most often is 'why don't you just use VS Code?' It's a reasonable question. VS Code is excellent. It has a massive ecosystem, great defaults, and you can be productive in it within minutes of installing it."

### 2. Sho - Development (Deepen Understanding Through Detail)

Build on the introduction with more detail, examples, or specifics. You're developing the reader's understanding of the subject within the frame established in Ki.

- Go deeper, not sideways — stay in the same frame
- Add specifics, stories, or observations that enrich the Ki
- The reader should feel like they're getting a thorough understanding of the topic
- This is NOT where you introduce the twist — resist the urge

**Example:** "Over the years I've tried to give honest answers. Muscle memory. Modal editing feels natural to me now. The terminal integration. The composability of keybindings. I usually mention that I've invested so much time configuring it that switching would mean abandoning years of customization. All true. All reasonable. And for about a decade, I believed that was the full answer."

### 3. Ten - Twist (Unexpected Recontextualization)

The turn. Introduce something that reframes everything from Ki and Sho without invalidating it. This is NOT a conflict or a problem — it's a shift in perspective that makes the reader see the same subject differently.

- The twist should surprise but not contradict
- "I realized" or "then I noticed" rather than "but then everything broke"
- The reader's reaction should be "oh" not "uh oh"
- This is the heart of the framework — spend time here

**Example:** "Last month my daughter watched me work for a few minutes. She's seven. She asked why I was 'typing in the black box' instead of using a 'normal computer.' And I realized the real answer to 'why Vim?' has nothing to do with efficiency or keybindings. It's that Vim is the only place in my digital life where I feel like I'm the one in control. Every other tool I use — my phone, my browser, even VS Code — is designed to suggest, to surface, to helpfully anticipate what I might want. Vim doesn't anticipate anything. It waits. It does exactly what I tell it to do, nothing more. In a world that's increasingly trying to predict my next move, Vim is the place where I still get to decide."

### 4. Ketsu - Reconciliation (Unify Both Perspectives)

Bring the original frame and the twist together into a unified understanding. The reader now holds both perspectives simultaneously — the subject is richer than it was before.

- Don't discard Ki/Sho — integrate them with Ten
- The original understanding was true; now it's also incomplete
- End with the expanded view, not a call to action

**Example:** "So the next time someone asks me why I don't use VS Code, I'll probably still say muscle memory and modal editing. Those reasons are real. But underneath them is something I didn't have words for until a seven-year-old made me think about it: I use Vim because it's the last tool I have that doesn't try to be smarter than me. Whether that's a feature or a bug probably depends on who you ask. For me, it's the whole point."

## Structure Notes

The four acts should feel like a natural unfolding, not a four-part argument. Ki and Sho are the reader settling into understanding. Ten is the moment the ground shifts. Ketsu is the new ground solidifying.

Pacing: Ki is short (one or two paragraphs). Sho is the longest section — it's doing the work of deepening understanding that makes the twist meaningful. Ten is medium-length — long enough to fully develop the recontextualization. Ketsu is short, like Ki, creating symmetry.

The critical distinction from Western frameworks: there is no antagonist. Nobody is wrong. No problem needs solving. The complication in SCQA is "something went wrong." The twist in Kishotenketsu is "I hadn't seen it from this angle before." If you find yourself framing the twist as a problem, you've slipped into a different framework.

The twist must be genuinely surprising but also genuinely earned by the development in Sho. If a reader can predict Ten from Ki, the post loses its power. If Ten comes out of nowhere without roots in Sho, it feels random.

## Combination Notes

- **The False Start:** The twist can subvert the entire setup, making the reader's initial frame the "false start" that gets recontextualized.
- **Progressive Disclosure:** The Sho section can use progressive disclosure to layer complexity before the twist reframes it all.
- **The Sparkline:** The twist is a sparkline turn — the shift between what-is and what-could-be. You can use sparkline pacing within the Sho section to build toward it.
- **Converging Ideas:** If the twist is a second thread connecting to the first, you're hybridizing Kishotenketsu with Converging Ideas. The twist connects rather than confronts.
- **The Spiral:** If you revisit the subject multiple times with increasing recontextualization, you're spiraling through multiple Ki-Sho-Ten-Ketsu cycles.

## When NOT to Use This

If your post has a clear problem and solution, use SCQA or PAS. Kishotenketsu is for reframing, not problem-solving. Forcing a reframing structure onto a problem-solving post makes the twist feel like you're burying the lede.

Also avoid this when you need to persuade through argument. Kishotenketsu persuades through perspective shift, which is gentler but slower. If you're writing "why you should use X," direct frameworks work better. If you're writing "here's how I came to see X differently," that's Kishotenketsu.

Posts where the reader needs to take specific action are a poor fit. Kishotenketsu changes how you see; it doesn't tell you what to do. If you need a clear call to action, end with a different framework's conclusion.
